About the Bougainville Natural Resource Hub

The islands of Bougainville and the adjacent Buka, together with several outlying small islands and atolls, form the Autonomous Region of Bougainville, Papua New Guinea.

The islands are abundant in natural resources, with volcanic soil producing rich and high-value agricultural products such as cocoa and vanilla, large swathes of primary and secondary forest, beche-de-mer, off-shore tuna fisheries and minerals. Careful management to ensure the healthy and sustainable lives and livelihoods will require the transformation of global level data into local level insights, and insights into actionable policy and programming.

The Bougainville Natural Resource Hub

Following the results of the December 2019 independence referendum, the Autonomous Region of Bougainville faces a wide array of critical sustainability challenges and strategic choices on its path to independence. The overall aim of this web catalogue, developed and maintained by UNDP, is to provide technical infrastructure, spatial data and analytics, framed in part by the Sustainable Development Goals and other strategic priorities, which can provide information and opportunities to assist the Autonomous Government of Bougainville in taking sound natural resource management decisions.

The Bougainville Natural Resource Hub is designed to be:

1. A Portal for accessing information on Natural Resource Management (NRM)

2. A Space where specialist organizations, academic institutions and ongoing local projects can share data and updates

3. A Decision-support tool for policy matters, civil society organizations and other stakeholders who want to monitor NRM data, and turn commitment into action

This web portal is based on the GeoNode open-source geospatial infrastructure.
The curriculum will be made available for technical focal points to learn how to upload, use, and develop data on this website.

The Challenge

The digital revolution is innovating the way we can measure and understand the environment. All environmental problems happen somewhere, and understanding the where can help us understand the why and the how for sustainable interventions. And now, through satellite imagery we have the ability to measure deforestation to the single tree; or record coral bleaching events in near real-time. Many of these datasets are free, produced by premier research institutions, and available for uptake into insights and planning, for example through portals such as the UN Biodiversity Lab, Global Forest Watch, and the World Environment Situation Room.

The Opportunity

Despite this, gaps remain between data and policy. Whilst data exists, policymakers need easy access to localized, actionable, and targeted analytics with which to take sound decisions, particularly in regards to natural resources. Through the aggregation of global, high-quality datasets with locally created data and insights, natural resource governance processes can become more transparent for all stakeholders by ensuring everyone has access to the same set of information. Spatial data represents a particularly engaging format of using data for decision-making, as maps have the ability to transcend language and cultural barriers.
